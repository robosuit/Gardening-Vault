# 🌿 Basil

## 🌱 Botanical Information

**Scientific Name:** *Ocimum basilicum* (sweet basil) and related species

**Family:** Lamiaceae (Mint family)

**Common Names:** Basil, sweet basil, common basil, garden basil

**Life Cycle:** Annual (completes lifecycle in one growing season)

**USDA Hardiness:** Sensitive to frost; dies at freezing temperatures

---

## 📏 Growth Profile

**Mature Height:** 12–24 inches (depends on variety and pinching practices)

**Mature Spread:** 12–18 inches

**Growth Rate:** Fast (visible daily growth once established)

**Time to Maturity:** 
- From seed: 6–8 weeks transplant-ready
- From transplant: 3–4 weeks to first harvest
- Peak productivity: July–September

**Leaf Production Rate:** Fast (can harvest continuously)

---

## ☀️ Cultivation Requirements

**Sun Exposure:** Full sun (6+ hours) preferred, minimum 4 hours

**Soil Type:** 
- Rich, well-draining loam
- Add compost/organic matter (loves fertility)
- Avoid heavy clay
- pH: Neutral (6.0–7.0), tolerates to 6.5–7.5

**Moisture:** 
- Consistent (keep moist, not waterlogged)
- Doesn't tolerate dryness (leaves drop, plants wilt, stress bolts)
- Water: 1–2 inches per week (more in heat)
- From below preferred (avoid wetting foliage)

**Temperature:** 
- Loves warmth (75–85°F ideal)
- Frost-sensitive (kills at 32°F)
- Won't thrive below 60°F
- Heat-tolerant (excellent summer performer)

**Hardiness:** NOT hardy; replant annually

**Spacing:** 12–18 inches apart (dwarf varieties closer)

**Planting Depth:** Plant at same depth as nursery pot

## 🌿 Growing Notes

### Starting from Seed

**Characteristics:**
- Tiny seeds (viable 5–6 years)
- High germination rate (85–95%)
- Fast germination (5–10 days typical)
- Warm germinator (prefers 75–80°F)

**Indoor Start (Recommended):**
- **Timing:** March–April (6–8 weeks before planting outdoors)
- **Medium:** Seed-starting mix
- **Light:** Needs light (surface sow or barely cover)
- **Temperature:** 75–80°F optimal (heating mat helpful)
- **Germination:** 5–7 days (fast!)
- **After sprouting:** Provide 14–16 hours light daily
- **Thinning:** Thin to one per cell once true leaves emerge
- **Fertilizing:** Light fertilizer once true leaves appear (optional)
- **Transplant outdoors:** After last frost, soil warm (April 20+ typical)
- **Hardening off:** 1–2 weeks gradual acclimation critical (basil tender)

**Direct Sowing Outdoors:**
- **Timing:** Late April–May (soil must be 70°F+)
- **Method:** Direct sow to garden (shallow cover)
- **Spacing:** Thin to 12 inches apart
- **Risk:** Later than indoor start (extends heat season for basil)

### Transplanting Outdoors

**Critical:** Soil must be WARM (65–70°F minimum, 75°F+ preferred)
- Cold soil = root rot, yellowing, slow growth
- Wait for consistent warm weather
- Don't rush (April early = too cold)
- Late April or early May typical

**Hardening Off Process:**
- Week 1: Shade, sheltered, 2–3 hours outdoors
- Week 2: Increasing light, 4–6 hours outdoors
- Week 3: Morning sun, afternoon shade, most of day
- Week 4: Ready for transplanting (after last frost + warm soil)

**Planting:**
1. Gently remove from cell pack
2. Loosen roots
3. Plant at same depth
4. Water well
5. Mulch 1–2 inches (keeps soil warm + consistent)
6. Water again after planting

### Seasonal Care

**Spring (April–May):**
- Germinate seeds/transplant after frost
- Water regularly (weekly establish)
- Pinch tops early to encourage bushiness
  - When plant has 6–8 true leaves, pinch center top
  - Stimulates branching 2–3x per pinch point
  - Results in bushy, productive plant vs. tall, lean
- Mulch 2 inches (warmth + moisture consistency)
- Fertilize lightly (basil is medium feeder)

**Summer (June–August):**
- Peak growth + harvest
- Pinch continuously (harvest = pruning)
  - Pinch ¼ inch above leaf node
  - Leaves grow back doubled
  - Remove flower buds (unless want seed; flowers divert energy)
- Water consistently (heat = high water demand)
- Fertilize every 2–3 weeks (light feed, fish emulsion or balanced)
- Heat-loving; thrives in summer stress
- Multiple harvests per week possible

**Fall (September–October):**
- Still productive (loves warm temps)
- First frost risk late October (Zone 7B Oct 25)
- Plant still productive through early October
- Frost kills absolutely (don't bother protecting)
- Harvest heavily before frost
- Make pesto, dry, or tincture in preparation

### Bolting (Flowering)

**Bolting behavior:** Basil flowers in response to:
- Temperature drop below 60°F (signal to reproduce)
- Day length shortening (late summer/fall)
- Water stress (irregular watering)
- Letting flowers set

**Managing bolting:**
- Pinch off flower buds (once appear)
- Keep watered consistently (prevents stress-bolting)
- Plant late (post-May) for longer pre-bolt season
- Enjoy flowers if you quit harvesting; acceptable

**Flowers usage:** 
- Edible (mild basil flavor)
- Attractive
- Bees love
- Can be dried
- If you're fine with plant done after flowering, let bolt

## ✂️ Harvest & Processing

### Harvesting Guidelines

**Timing:**
- Begin: Plant 6–8 inches tall, at least 6 leaves
- Frequency: Every 3–5 days (continuous harvest)
- Season: June–frost (late October)

**Method:**
1. Pinch tops 1/4 inch ABOVE a leaf node
2. Leaves regrow from where pinched (2–3 new branches form)
3. Pinching = harvest + pruning simultaneously

**What to Pinch:**
- Flower buds (first priority)
- Growing tips (encourage bushiness)
- Outer leaves (leave center growing)

**Fresh Use:**
- Use immediately (3–7 days fresh in fridge if necessary)
- Tear gently (bruising releases bitter compounds)
- No cooking usually (use fresh or barely warm)

### Drying Basil

**Timing:** Peak potency June–August

**Method 1: Air Drying**
1. Bundle stems (6–10 stems)
2. Hang upside down in cool, dark, airy location
3. 1–2 weeks drying
4. Leaves should be crispy
5. Strip leaves from stems
6. Store in sealed jars

**Method 2: Dehydrator**
1. Strip leaves from stems
2. Arrange on dehydrator trays
3. 105°F, 4–6 hours
4. Leaves crispy when done
5. Cool, store sealed

**Note:** Dried basil loses some flavor vs. fresh; use in cooking rather than fresh applications

### Pesto (Best Preservation)

**Recipe:**
- 2 cups fresh basil leaves (packed)
- ½ cup olive oil
- ½ cup parmesan cheese (or pine nuts for vegan)
- 3–4 cloves garlic
- Salt + pepper

**Process:**
1. Blend basil, garlic, nuts (optional: pine nuts add richness)
2. Add oil slowly, blending
3. Fold in cheese
4. Freeze in ice cube trays (perfect portions)
5. Pop out frozen; store in freezer bags
6. Lasts 6+ months frozen

**Culinary use:** Thaw + use in pasta, sandwiches, dips

### Storage

**Fresh leaves:** 3–7 days refrigerated (in damp paper towel, sealed bag)

**Dried (loose):** 6–12 months in sealed jars, cool dark place

**Pesto (frozen):** 6+ months in freezer

## 🌈 Appearance

**Color:** 
- Green (most varieties: bright to dark green)
- Purple varieties (dark purple foliage, pink flowers)
- Red varieties (rare; burgundy foliage)

**Foliage:**
- Oval leaves, 1–3 inches long
- Opposite leaf arrangement
- Smooth texture (some varieties slightly fuzzy)
- Bright, fresh appearance
- Aromatic when brushed

**Flowers:**
- Tiny, tubular, white to pink to purple
- Clustered in spikes at branch ends
- If allowed to flower, quite showy
- Attractive to bees + pollinators

**Fragrance:**
- Intensely aromatic
- Distinctive basil scent (pungent, sweet, warm)
- Strongest in fresh leaves
- Released by crushing or brushing (volatile oils)

## 🧪 Preparation Methods

### 🍵 Tea Infusion

**Best For:** Mild digestive support, warm beverage

**Ratio:** 1–2 tsp fresh (or ½ tsp dried) per 8 oz hot water

**Steep:** 5–10 minutes (short steep; longer = bitter)

**Frequency:** 1–2 cups daily or after meals

**Taste:** Warm, pungent, slightly sweet, distinctive

**Best:** Sipped warm

**Note:** More commonly used fresh or in cooking than as formal tea

### 🍽️ Culinary Use (Fresh)

**Best For:** Daily consumption through cooking/fresh use

**Applications:**
- Pesto (basil + garlic + oil + nuts)
- Fresh in salads (tear gently)
- Over pasta (cooked or fresh)
- In soups (add near end)
- Sprinkled on tomato dishes (classic pairing)
- In herbal waters (basil-infused water)

**Dosage:** Use freely (culinary amounts safe, health-promoting)

**Benefit:** Regular consumption through food = continuous gentle medicinal benefit

### 🧪 Tincture

**Best For:** Concentrated, longer shelf life

**Ratio:** 1:2 fresh basil (or 1:5 dried) in 40% alcohol

**Maceration:** 4 weeks

**Dose:** 10–20 drops, 2–3x daily

**Benefit:** Concentrated action, shelf-stable

**Note:** Less common than fresh use (fresh superior for basil's benefits)

### 🧴 Infused Oil

**Best For:** Digestive massage oil, culinary oil

**Method:**
1. Infuse fresh basil in olive oil (2–3 weeks, cool dark place)
2. Don't heat (preserves volatile oils)
3. Strain thoroughly (remove all plant matter)
4. Bottle, label

**Use:** 
- Culinary drizzle
- Gentle abdominal massage (digestive support)
- Storage: 3–6 months

## ⚠️ Safety & Contraindications

**Generally Safe:** Yes, excellent safety profile

**Allergies:** Rare but possible (Lamiaceae family or specific basil)

**Drug Interactions:** None known

**Pregnancy:** Culinary amounts safe; large medicinal amounts (tinctures) best discussed with provider

**Breastfeeding:** Safe in culinary amounts

**Contraindications:** None significant

**Cautions:**
- May cause dermatitis in sensitive individuals (handling)
- Contact with eyes: Rinse if irritated
- Some sources note uterine stimulation potential (very large amounts only)

**Consult Healthcare Provider If:**
- Pregnant (medicinal dosing)
- Taking anticoagulants (theoretical interaction, minor)
- History of basil family allergy
