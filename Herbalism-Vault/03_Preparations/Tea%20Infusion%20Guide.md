# 🍵 Tea Infusion Guide

A complete guide to preparing herbal infusions, the most accessible and popular herbal preparation method.

---

## What is an Infusion?

An infusion is a preparation where fresh or dried herbs are steeped in hot water, allowing water-soluble compounds (polysaccharides, flavonoids, some volatile oils) to extract into the water.

**Best for:** Leaves, flowers, soft aerial parts

**Not ideal for:** Roots, bark, seeds, tough plant material (use decoction instead)

---

## Basic Infusion Recipe

### Ingredients
- 1 tbsp dried herb OR 2–3 tbsp fresh herb
- 8 oz freshly boiled water
- Optional: honey, lemon, sweetener

### Method

1. **Boil water** to 200–212°F (just-boiled)
2. **Add herb** to cup or teapot
3. **Pour water** over herb
4. **Steep** 10–15 minutes (see notes below for timing by herb)
5. **Strain** thoroughly to remove all plant material
6. **Drink** warm or room temperature

### Yield
1 standard cup (8 oz)

---

## Steeping Times by Herb Type

### Delicate Flowers (10–12 minutes)
- [[Chamomile]]
- [[Lavender]]
- [[Lemon Balm]] (mild steep okay)
- [[Bergamot]] (flowers)

**Note:** Longer steeping = more bitter compounds, potentially less pleasant

### Standard Leaf/Aerial Parts (12–15 minutes)
- [[Peppermint]]
- [[Thyme]]
- [[Oregano]]
- [[Lemon Balm]]
- [[Sage]]
- [[Marjoram]]
- [[Catnip]]

**Notes:** These tolerate longer steeping well; flavor deepens

### Robust Herbs (15+ minutes, or can simmer)
- [[Hyssop]]
- [[Horehound]]
- [[Thyme]] (woody parts)

**Notes:** Can be simmered lightly 5 minutes, then steeped remaining time

## Dried vs. Fresh Herb Ratios

### Dried Herbs
**Standard:** 1 tbsp per 8 oz water  
**Strong brew:** 1.5–2 tbsp per 8 oz water  
**Mild brew:** ½–¾ tbsp per 8 oz water

**Dried herbs are more concentrated:**
- Drying reduces water content
- Compounds become more concentrated
- 1 tbsp dried ≈ 2–3 tbsp fresh

### Fresh Herbs
**Standard:** 2–3 tbsp per 8 oz water (loosely packed)  
**Strong brew:** 3–4 tbsp per 8 oz water  
**Mild brew:** 1–2 tbsp per 8 oz water

**Fresh herbs are more dilute:**
- Higher water content
- Compounds less concentrated
- More volume needed for same strength

## Temperature Guidelines

### Just-Boiled (200–212°F)
**Best for:** Most herbs  
**Benefit:** Extracts maximum compounds, quick steeping  
**Herbs:** Chamomile, Lavender, Peppermint, most leaves

### Cooling Water (175°F, after 5 minutes)
**Best for:** Very delicate flowers  
**Benefit:** Preserves delicate volatile oils, gentler  
**Herbs:** Jasmine, some rose varieties (if growing)

### Room Temperature (for already-prepared tea)
**How:** Steep as usual, then cool  
**Best:** Summer cold infusions, traveling  
**Benefit:** Refreshing, different extraction profile

## Infusion Strength Guide

### Mild Infusion
**Ratio:** 1 tsp dried herb per 8 oz water  
**Steep:** 5–10 minutes  
**Use:** Daily maintenance, delicate herbs, tea for pleasure  
**Taste:** Subtle, light flavor  

### Standard Infusion
**Ratio:** 1 tbsp dried herb per 8 oz water  
**Steep:** 12–15 minutes  
**Use:** Medicinal support, therapeutic dose  
**Taste:** Noticeable herbal flavor  

### Strong Infusion
**Ratio:** 1.5–2 tbsp dried herb per 8 oz water  
**Steep:** 15–20 minutes  
**Use:** Acute medicinal need, concentrated effect  
**Taste:** Bold, herbal, possibly bitter  

### Extra-Strong (Medicinal)
**Ratio:** 2–3 tbsp dried herb per 4–6 oz water  
**Steep:** 20–30 minutes  
**Use:** Maximum medicinal effect, shorter dose time  
**Taste:** Very pronounced, frequently bitter  
**Note:** May taste unpleasant; consider mixing with honey or combining herbs

## Single-Herb vs. Herbal Blends

### Single-Herb Infusion

**Advantages:**
- Simple, pure
- Easy to assess how you respond
- Focused medicinal action
- One flavor profile

**Best for:**
- [[Chamomile]] (calming)
- [[Peppermint]] (digestion)
- [[Lavender]] (relaxation)

### Herbal Blend (2–4 herbs)

**Advantages:**
- More complex flavor
- Multiple medicinal actions
- Synergistic effect (herbals work better combined)
- More interesting taste

**Best Blends:**

**Digestive Blend:**
- 2 parts [[Peppermint]]
- 1 part [[Chamomile]]
- 1 part [[Dill]] or [[Fennel]]

**Calming Blend:**
- 2 parts [[Lavender]]
- 2 parts [[Lemon Balm]]
- 1 part [[Chamomile]]

**Respiratory Blend:**
- 2 parts [[Thyme]]
- 1 part [[Horehound]]
- 1 part [[Hyssop]]

## Storage & Shelf Life

### Fresh-Made Infusion
**Storage:** Refrigerated  
**Shelf life:** 3–5 days  
**Best:** Drink fresh, within 24 hours ideally  
**Note:** Compounds degrade over time; fresh is most potent

### Dried Herbs (for infusing)
**Storage:** Sealed glass or paper bags, cool dark place  
**Shelf life:** 1–2 years  
**Optimal:** Within 1 year for maximum potency  
**Signs of age:** Pale color, weak aroma, reduced effect

### Herbal Blend (pre-mixed)
**Storage:** Sealed glass jars, cool dark cupboard  
**Shelf life:** 6–12 months (blends lose potency faster than single herbs)  
**Best:** Prepare blends in small batches

## Making Infusions Cold (Sun Tea Method)

### Cold Infusion Method

**Time:** 6–12 hours (overnight is convenient)

**Process:**
1. Place 1 tbsp dried herb in closed jar
2. Add 8 oz cool water
3. Leave at room temperature or in sun 6–12 hours
4. Strain thoroughly

**Advantages:**
- Gentler extraction
- Preserves delicate volatile oils
- Can be left overnight without attention
- Refreshing served cold

**Disadvantages:**
- Longer preparation time
- Slightly less extraction of some compounds
- Requires advance planning

**Best herbs for cold infusion:**
- [[Mint]] family (especially refreshing cold)
- [[Chamomile]] (gentle extraction preserves delicate compounds)
- [[Lavender]]

## Herbal Infusion Dosage

### Daily Maintenance (Wellness)
**Dose:** 1 cup of standard infusion, 1–2x daily  
**Best:** Regular consistent use  
**Example:** [[Peppermint]] tea daily for digestive wellness  

### Therapeutic Dose (Addressing Specific Issue)
**Dose:** 1–3 cups of standard strength, 2–3x daily  
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks or as needed  
**Example:** [[Lavender]] tea 3x daily for stress support  

### Acute Support (Sudden Need)
**Dose:** 1 cup strong infusion, every 2–4 hours as needed  
**Duration:** Until acute situation resolves (usually 3–7 days)  
**Example:** [[Thyme]] tea for respiratory comfort during cold

## Infusion Additions for Taste & Function

### Honey
- 1–2 tsp per cup
- Adds sweetness, soothing, antimicrobial
- Add after cooling slightly (heat destroys honey enzymes)
- Pairing: Works with all herbs

### Lemon Juice
- ½–1 tbsp per cup
- Brightens flavor, aids mineral absorption
- Assists with iron absorption (if herbal iron-rich)
- Pairing: Peppermint, Chamomile, Lavender

### Ginger (from spice cupboard)
- Small slice or ¼ tsp powder
- Warming, improves circulation, aids digestion
- Pairing: Digestive blends, respiratory blends

### Apple Cider Vinegar (1–2 tsp)
- Slightly sour, mineral-rich
- Enhances mineral extraction
- Pairing: Nutritive infusions, digestive blends

### Cinnamon (from spice cupboard)
- Pinch to ¼ tsp
- Warming, blood-sugar supporting
- Pairing: Digestive blends, warming infusions

### Cayenne Pepper (pinch)
- Stimulating to circulation
- Warming
- Pairing: Respiratory blends, warming digestive

## Common Questions

**Q: Can I re-steep the same herbs?**

**A:** Yes, once. The first infusion extracts 70–80% of compounds. Second steeping gets remaining. Usually add 1–2 minutes longer. Third steeping generally too weak to be worthwhile.

---

**Q: Can I make a large batch to drink over several days?**

**A:** Not recommended. Fresh infusions lose potency daily. Better to brew fresh daily, or make tinctures for longer storage.

---

**Q: What if my tea tastes too bitter?**

**A:** 
- Reduce steeping time (over-steeping = bitterness)
- Use less herb
- Add honey or lemon
- Blend with milder herb (add Chamomile)

---

**Q: Is leaving herbs steeping overnight okay?**

**A:** Generally okay, but potency decreases. Flavor may become unpleasant. Better to brew fresh or use tincture for longer-lasting strength.

## Herbal Tea Ritual

Making herbal infusions can be more than functional:

1. **Slow down:** Take 5 minutes to mindfully prepare tea
2. **Notice:** Observe color, aroma, steam
3. **Sip intentionally:** Warm cup in hands, small sips
4. **Feel:** Notice warmth, taste, subtle effects
5. **Appreciate:** Gratitude for plant medicine

Many herbalists find this ritualistic aspect part of the healing power.
