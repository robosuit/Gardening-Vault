# 🧪 Tincture Guide

A complete guide to making and using herbal tinctures, concentrated extracts that preserve herbal medicine effectively for months to years.

---

## What is a Tincture?

A tincture is an alcohol-based extract where herbs are macerated (soaked) in alcohol, extracting water-soluble and oil-soluble compounds into the liquid. The result is a shelf-stable, concentrated herbal preparation.

**Advantages:**
- Long shelf life (3–10 years)
- Highly concentrated (small dose = strong effect)
- Fast absorption (taken sublingually)
- Preserves volatile oils better than water infusions
- Easy to travel with

**Best for:** Most herbs, especially potent herbs

**Not ideal for:** Large-quantity daily use (cost inefficient vs. tea)

---

## Basic Tincture Chemistry

### Alcohol as Extractant

**Why alcohol?**
- Extracts both water-soluble and oil-soluble compounds
- Acts as preservative (prevents spoilage)
- Faster absorption (small dose, concentrated effect)
- Long shelf life

**Alcohol strengths:**
- **High proof (95–190):** Best for fresh herbs, drugs
- **Moderate proof (80):** Best for dried herbs, balanced extraction
- **Lower proof (40–60):** Gentler, suitable for children/sensitive individuals

### Extraction Ratios

These indicate herb weight to alcohol volume:

**1:2 Ratio (Fresh Herbs)**
- 1 pound fresh herb : 2 quarts alcohol
- More concentrated
- For very potent fresh herbs
- Burns/stimulating plants

**1:5 Ratio (Dried Herbs)**
- 1 pound dried herb : 5 quarts alcohol
- Standard ratio for dried material
- Most common, easiest
- General herbs

**1:10 Ratio (Very Gentle)**
- 1 pound dried herb : 10 quarts alcohol
- Most dilute
- For delicate herbs or sensitive individuals
- Children's tinctures

## Tincture Making: Step-by-Step

### Equipment Needed

**Essential:**
- Glass jar with tight-fitting lid (quart-sized or larger)
- Strainer or fine mesh colander
- Cheesecloth or coffee filters
- Measuring cups & spoons
- Bottles for storage (dark glass, dropper bottles preferred)
- Labels

**Optional:**
- Kitchen scale (for accuracy)
- Mortar & pestle (for breaking up herbs)
- Coffee grinder (fine grinding)

---

### Method 1: Fresh Herb Tincture (1:2 Ratio)

**Best for:** [[Hyssop]], [[Echinacea]], [[Catnip]], [[Lemon Balm]] fresh

#### Ingredients
- Fresh herb, finely chopped (enough to fill jar halfway)
- High-proof alcohol (95–190 proof, like everclear) to cover herbs by 2 inches

#### Process

1. **Chop herb finely** to increase surface area for extraction

2. **Pack jar** loosely with herb material

3. **Pour alcohol** over herbs until covered by 2 inches

4. **Seal jar tightly** with lid

5. **Label jar** with:
   - Herb name
   - Plant part (leaf, root, flower)
   - Type of alcohol
   - Date started
   - Target finish date (4–6 weeks)

6. **Macerate** (soak) in cool, dark place for 4–6 weeks
   - Shake jar vigorously 1–2x daily (increases extraction)
   - After first 2 weeks, can reduce to once daily

7. **Strain thoroughly** after 4–6 weeks:
   - Pour through strainer lined with cheesecloth
   - Press herb material to extract remaining liquid
   - Discard herb matter
   - Filter through fine strainer again if cloudiness remains

8. **Bottle & store**:
   - Use dark glass bottles with dropper caps
   - Label with herb, ratio, and date made
   - Store in cool, dark place

#### Shelf Life
5–10 years (alcohol preserves indefinitely)

---

### Method 2: Dried Herb Tincture (1:5 Ratio)

**Best for:** Most dried herbs you've grown or purchased

#### Ingredients
- Dried herb (enough to fill jar 1/3)
- Moderate-proof alcohol (80 proof, like vodka, or higher)

#### Process

1. **Measure herb**:
   - If using scale: 1 oz dried herb per 5 oz alcohol
   - By volume: Fill 1/3 of jar with herb

2. **Break up herbs** slightly (don't powder):
   - Rough chop or crush slightly
   - Increases surface area

3. **Add alcohol**:
   - Pour until herbs covered by 2 inches
   - Dried herbs absorb liquid; fill generously

4. **Seal & label** (same as fresh method)

5. **Macerate** 4–weeks:
   - Shake daily for first 2 weeks
   - Shake 1–2x per week after
   - Watch alcohol level (dried herbs expand then compress)
   - Add more alcohol if herbs absorb and become exposed

6. **Strain** (same as fresh):
   - After 4 weeks, strain thoroughly
   - Press herb material to extract
   - Filter if cloudy

7. **Bottle & store** in dark glass bottles

#### Shelf Life
5–10 years

## Alcohol Selection

### Everclear (95–190 proof)
**Pros:** Highest extraction, long shelf life, potent  
**Cons:** Not available everywhere (some states), taste harsh, too strong for young children  
**Best for:** Potent fresh herbs, professional-strength tinctures  

### Vodka (80 proof)
**Pros:** Readily available, neutral taste, appropriate alcohol content for dried herbs, reasonable cost  
**Cons:** Slightly less extraction than high-proof, doesn't preserve quite as long  
**Best for:** Standard herb tinctures, dried herbs, most accessible option  

### Brandy (80 proof)
**Pros:** Readily available, nice taste, good for herbs meant for pleasure (nervines, digestive)  
**Cons:** Doesn't extract quite as well as neutral spirits, cost  
**Best for:** Culinary/medicinal blends meant to taste pleasant  

### Whiskey/Bourbon (80+ proof)
**Pros:** Available, adds warming property  
**Cons:** Flavor dominates  
**Best for:** Warming herbs, if you like whiskey taste  

### NOT Recommended
- Wine (too low alcohol, spoils easily)
- Beer (too low alcohol)
- Store-brand extracts (often not pure alcohol)

## Finished Tincture Dosage

### Standard Dose

**For adults:**
- **Acute (sudden need):** 20–30 drops, every 2–4 hours
- **Therapeutic (regular use):** 20–30 drops, 2–3x daily
- **Prevention/maintenance:** 20 drops, 1x daily

### Dose Administration

**Sublingually (under tongue):**
- Fastest absorption (2–3 minutes to bloodstream)
- Most efficient
- Hold under tongue 30 seconds before swallowing
- Takes: Strong taste, burn for high-proof tinctures

**In water:**
- Add dose to 2 oz warm water
- Drink quickly
- Masks taste, slower absorption
- Good for children

**In juice or tea:**
- Mix into favorite beverage
- Completely masks herbal taste
- Slightly slower absorption
- Most palatable

### Children's Dosing

**General rule:** 1 drop per year of age

- **2-year-old:** 2 drops
- **5-year-old:** 5 drops
- **12-year-old:** 12 drops
- **Adolescent/adult:** 20–30 drops

**Or use glycerin tinctures (alcohol-free) for young children**

## Testing & Quality

### Signs of Good Tincture
✓ Darker color (extraction occurred)  
✓ Strong herbal aroma  
✓ Taste matches herb (bitter, pungent, etc.)  
✓ Clear (or slightly cloudy, both fine)  
✓ No mold, cloudiness after months (alcohol prevents)  

### Signs of Problem/Bad Tincture
✗ Pale/light color (under-extracted)  
✗ No aroma (weak)  
✗ Moldy smell or taste  
✗ Separated into layers (failed extraction)  
✗ Cloudiness with moisture inside bottle

## Tincture Shelf Life & Storage

### Optimal Storage
- **Temperature:** Cool (50–70°F), consistent
- **Light:** Dark place (dark glass bottles help)
- **Container:** Glass, not plastic (alcohol dissolves some plastics)
- **Seal:** Airtight, dropper cap

### Shelf Life
- **High-proof (95+ alcohol):** 10+ years
- **Standard (80 proof):** 5–10 years
- **Low-proof (40–60):** 3–5 years

**Note:** Effectiveness may diminish slightly over years, but generally remains viable for many years if properly stored.

## Popular Tinctures for Herbalists

### Digestive Blend Tincture
**Herbs:**
- 2 parts dried [[Fennel]] seed
- 1 part dried [[Ginger]]
- 1 part dried Peppermint
- ½ part [[Caraway]] seed

**Dose:** 20 drops before meals  
**Use:** Digestive support, prevents bloating

**Herbs:**
- 2 parts dried [[Thyme]]
- 1 part dried [[Horehound]]
- 1 part dried [[Echinacea]] root
- ½ part dried [[Ginger]]

**Ratio:** 1:5 (dried) in vodka  
**Dose:** 30 drops, 3–4x daily during respiratory support

**Herbs:**
- 2 parts dried [[Lavender]]
- 1 part dried [[Lemon Balm]]
- 1 part dried [[Catnip]]
- ½ part [[Peppermint]]

**Ratio:** 1:5 (dried) in brandy (nicer taste)

- 2 parts fresh [[Echinacea]] (leaf + root)

**Ratio:** 1:2 (fresh) in high-proof alcohol  
**Dose:** 30 drops, 3–4x daily during infection risk  
**Use:** Seasonal immune support

## Making Alcohol-Free Tinctures (Glycerin)

**Alternative for:**
- Young children
- Alcohol-free preference
- Religious/health reasons

### Method

**Ingredients:**
- Dried herb (1 part by weight)
- Vegetable glycerin (5 parts by volume)

**Process:**
1. Mix herb + glycerin in jar (glycerin dissolves more slowly, so use equal ratio or slightly more herb)
2. Seal tightly
3. Macerate 4–6 weeks, shake daily
4. Strain thoroughly
5. Bottle, label, store

**Dose:** Same as alcohol tinctures (20–30 drops)

**Shelf life:** 2–3 years (glycerin is more prone to fermentation over time)

**Taste:** Sweet, children often prefer

## Storage Bottles & Labels

### Ideal Bottle
- Dark amber or blue glass (blocks light)
- 1–2 oz with dropper cap
- Airtight, glass dropper (not plastic)
- Labeled with:
  - Herb name(s)
  - Ratio & alcohol type
  - Date made/finish date
  - Dosage instructions
  - Intended use

### Labeling Template
```
[[Herbal Name]] Tincture
1:5 Dried in Vodka
Date Made: [Month/Year]
Date to Use By: [Expiration]
Dose: 20–30 drops, 2–3x daily
Use: [Digestive/Calming/Respiratory/etc.]
Warnings: [Any specific cautions]
```

## Troubleshooting Tinctures

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Weak extraction (pale, no aroma)** | Under-maceration or low alcohol | Shake daily, macerate longer (6–8 weeks), use higher-proof alcohol next time |
| **Mold/cloudiness** | Low alcohol or contamination | Not normally viable; discard and start fresh |
| **Separation/layers** | Failed extraction or contamination | Discard; ensure jars are clean, alcohol is quality |
| **Evaporation** | Seal not tight or old alcohol | Check lid seals; store tightly; not dangerous, just more concentrated |
| **Sediment at bottom** | Fine plant particles | Normal; shake before use or filter through coffee filter |
