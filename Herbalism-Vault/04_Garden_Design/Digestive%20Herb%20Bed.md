# 🍽️ Digestive Herb Bed Design

A specialized garden bed focused on herbs that support digestive health, creating a functional apothecary garden dedicated to food as medicine.

---

## Digestive Herb Garden Purpose

This bed brings together plants traditionally used to support:
- Digestion and digestive function
- Carminative action (reduces gas/bloating)
- Antispasmodic effects (relaxes cramping)
- Bitter toning (stimulates digestive secretions)
- Anti-inflammatory action (soothes GI tract)

**Philosophy:** Grow digestive medicine you use daily, harvest fresh, use in meals and medicines.

---

## Core Digestive Herbs

### [[Fennel]]
- Height: 3–5 feet
- Spread: 18–24 inches
- Placement: Back (tall, narrow)
- Water: Low to medium
- Flavor: Sweet, licorice-like
- Best use: Seeds for tea, digestive support post-meal
- Blooms: Yellow tiny umbels attracting beneficial insects
- Perennial: Yes, lives 2–3 years typically

### [[Dill]]
- Height: 2–4 feet
- Spread: 12–18 inches
- Placement: Back to middle
- Water: Medium
- Flavor: Fresh, dill-distinctive
- Best use: Seeds in tea, fresh leaves in cooking
- Annual: Self-seeds readily
- Benefit: "Dill water" traditional for infant gas

### [[Anise]]
- Height: 18–24 inches
- Spread: 12 inches
- Placement: Middle
- Water: Medium
- Flavor: Sweet, similar to fennel (milder)
- Best use: Seeds for tea, culinary flavoring
- Annual: Must replant each year

### [[Peppermint]]
- Height: 2–3 feet (if trained)
- Spread: Aggressive (underground runners)
- Placement: Container ONLY or with barriers
- Water: Moist (loves water)
- Flavor: Cooling, refreshing, strong
- Best use: Fresh in meals, hot tea post-dinner
- Perennial: Comes back vigorously
- **CRITICAL:** Use 5-gallon pot or in-ground barrier to contain!

### [[Cilantro]]
- Height: 12–18 inches
- Spread: 8–12 inches
- Placement: Front to middle
- Water: Regular
- Flavor: Fresh, pungent, polarizing (love it or hate it)
- Best use: Fresh in meals, seeds for spice
- Annual: Cool-season herb
- Bolting: Bolts quickly in heat (succession sow)

### [[Caraway]]
- Height: 12–24 inches
- Spread: 12–18 inches
- Placement: Middle
- Water: Medium
- Flavor: Distinctive, slightly bitter, warming
- Best use: Seeds for tea, traditional spice
- Biennial: First year growth, second year flowering/seed
- Challenge: Takes 2 years to harvest seed

### [[Chamomile]]
- Height: 12–24 inches
- Spread: 12–18 inches
- Placement: Front to middle
- Water: Medium
- Flavor: Mild, slightly sweet, floral
- Best use: Flowers dried for tea, soothing
- Annual: Self-seeds readily
- Bonus: Anti-inflammatory GI lining

## Supporting Digestive Herbs

### [[Marjoram]]
- Height: 12–18 inches
- Placement: Front-middle
- Digestion: Mild action, warming
- Best use: Culinary herb, adds to digestive blends
- Flavor: Subtle, sweet

### [[Oregano]]
- Height: 12–18 inches
- Placement: Front-middle
- Digestion: Stimulates digestive secretions
- Best use: Culinary herb, fresh or dried
- Antimicrobial: Bonus benefit

### [[Summer Savory]]
- Height: 12–24 inches (depending on type)
- Placement: Middle
- Digestion: Excellent carminative
- Best use: Fresh with legumes (reduces gas!)
- Uses: Culinary (meat, beans), medicinal tea

## 📐 Digestive Bed Design (4' x 12')

```
BACK (NORTH)
[Fennel] [Dill] [Dill-2nd sowing]

MIDDLE
[Anise]  [Caraway]  [Summer Savory]  [Cilantro]

FRONT (SOUTH)
[Chamomile] [Marjoram] [Oregano]
```

**Total plants:** 12–15

**Note on Peppermint:** Plant separately in 5-gallon pot near (not in) the bed

**Seasonal consideration:** 
- Cilantro bolt quickly in heat (succession sow every 3 weeks March–April, August–September)
- Anise + Dill are annuals (replant each year)
- Fennel, Caraway, Herb perennials provide continuity

## Planting Timeline (Zone 7B)

### Early Spring (March–April)
- Direct sow: Cilantro, Chamomile, Caraway
- Plant: Peppermint in separate container
- Start indoors: Can be done but not necessary for most

### Late Spring (April–May)
- After last frost (April 15)
- Direct sow: Anise, Dill (multiple sowings 3 weeks apart)
- Plant: Any purchased herbs or starts

### Early Fall (August–September)
- Cilantro again: Prefers cool season
- Dill: Succession sow for fall harvest
- Chamomile: Can overseed for next year

### Winter
- Peppermint dormant in container
- Mulch perennials lightly
- Plan next year

## Succession Planting for Continuous Harvest

### Cilantro Strategy
- Sow cilantro every 2–3 weeks (March–April, August–September)
- This creates continuous supply
- Bolts quickly in summer heat (stop sowing June–July)
- Resumes August

### Dill Strategy
- First sowing: Late April
- Second sowing: Mid-May
- Third sowing: August–September
- Creates overlapping maturity for extended harvest

### Constant Fresh Leaves
- Early season: Fresh Cilantro, early Dill
- Summer: Peppermint (thrives in heat), Oregano, Marjoram, Summer Savory
- Fall: Cilantro + Dill second crops, Chamomile flowers

---

## Garden Care & Harvesting

### Spring Management
- Thin seedlings (crowded = weak plants)
- Water regularly (establishing)
- Pinch back leggy growth
- No harvesting first month (let establish)

### Summer Management
- Pinch flower buds if you want leaves (otherwise let some flower)
- Harvest from top 1/3 of plant
- Peppermint: Harvest vigorously (containment tactic)
- Cilantro: Will bolt; accept and harvest before OR pull + replant
- Regular harvesting = bushier plants

### Fall Management
- Harvest cilantro + dill second crops
- Reduce watering as temps cool
- Begin drying herbs for winter
- Chamomile flowers ready

### Winter Management
- Most dormant
- Peppermint minimal watering (in container)
- Plan: Order seeds for spring
- Dry harvested herbs

## Culinary & Medicinal Use

### Post-Meal Digestive Tea
**Recipe:**
- 1 tbsp fresh Fennel + Dill
- OR ½ tbsp each Caraway, Anise, Marjoram
- 1 cup hot water, steep 10–15 minutes

**Effect:** Reduces bloating, supports digestion, pleasant ritual

### Fresh-Herb Meal Approach
- Use Cilantro in salads, salsas
- Add Oregano, Marjoram, Summer Savory to meals
- Use Dill in fish dishes
- Consume herbs with meals for ongoing digestive support
- Culinary amounts = gentle, regular benefit

### Digestive Syrup
- Infuse Fennel + Ginger in oil (4 weeks)
- Make syrup with honey
- Take 1 tsp after meals
- Stronger than tea, pleasant taste

### Digestive Tincture
- Tincture Fennel + Caraway in alcohol (1:5 ratio)
- Take 20 drops before meals
- Concentrated, long shelf life

## Companion Planting with Digestive Guild

### Natural Groupings
The digestive herbs naturally support each other:
- Similar sun needs (full sun to partial shade)
- Seeds/flowers attract beneficial insects together
- Self-seeding creates natural renewal

### What to Avoid in This Bed
✗ Mints outside containers (will overrun)  
✗ Low-water Mediterranean herbs (water needs differ)  
✗ Shade-lovers (these need sun)  
✗ Heavy feeders (need balanced soil)  

### Good Nearby Additions (Not in Bed)
- Container of Peppermint (controlled)
- Container of Lemon Balm (adjacent nervine)
- Oregano (slight different guild but compatible)

## Soil & Watering

### Soil Preparation
- Loose, well-draining
- Rich with organic matter (compost)
- Slightly moist-preferring (vs. drought herbs)
- pH neutral (6.5–7.5)

### Watering Schedule
- Establishment (first month): 2–3x weekly (keep moist)
- Growing season (summer): 1–2x weekly depending on rainfall
- Not as drought-tolerant as Mediterranean herbs
- Don't over-water = root rot

### Mulching
- 2–3 inches straw or wood chips
- Helps retain moisture
- Keeps soil cooler (Cilantro appreciates this)
- Suppresses weeds

---

## Pest & Disease Management

### Prevention First
- Healthy plants resist problems
- Good air circulation (proper spacing)
- Water at soil level (not foliage)
- Remove diseased leaves promptly

### Common Issues
**Powdery mildew** (white coating)
- Cause: Moisture on foliage + humidity
- Prevention: Don't overhead water, space well
- Solution: Remove affected leaves, improve air flow

**Slugs/snails** (on Cilantro)
- Cause: Cool, moist conditions
- Prevention: Hand-pick in early morning
- Solution: Beer traps, copper tape (organic)
- Less issue in summer, more in spring/fall

**Aphids** (rare, usually on Dill)
- Prevention: Encourage beneficial insects (good habitat)
- Solution: Spray water forcefully (knocks off), neem oil
- Usually not needed; beneficial insects handle naturally

## Dried Herb Storage for Winter

### How to Dry
1. Harvest in morning after dew dries
2. Bundle with twine, hang in cool dark place
3. 2–3 weeks drying typical
4. Leaves should crumble when fully dry

### Storage
- Glass jars, sealed
- Cool, dark cupboard
- 1–2 years good quality
- Label with herb + date

### Winter Use
- Dried tea infusions (strong + shelf-stable)
- Tinctures (if making)
- Cooking
