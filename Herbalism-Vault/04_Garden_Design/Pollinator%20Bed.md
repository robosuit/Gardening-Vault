# 🐝 Pollinator Bed Design

A vibrant herb garden specifically designed to attract and support pollinators—bees, butterflies, hoverflies, and beneficial insects that sustain garden ecosystem health.

---

## Why a Pollinator Garden?

**Benefits:**
- ✓ Supports pollinator populations
- ✓ Improves seed set + fruit production in food garden
- ✓ Beautiful, colorful beds
- ✓ Diverse blooms throughout season
- ✓ Medicinal + culinary herbs at same time
- ✓ Creates habitat for beneficial insects
- ✓ Ecological contribution to local ecosystem

---

## Best Pollinator-Attracting Herbs

### [[Borage]]
- Height: 24–36 inches
- Flowers: Bright blue, star-shaped, abundant
- Blooms: June–September
- Bee value: EXCELLENT (bees love borage)
- Placement: Middle to back
- Special note: Self-seeds readily
- Uses: Edible flowers, refreshing taste

### [[Bergamot]]
- Height: 24–36 inches
- Flowers: Shaggy pink/red/purple spheres
- Blooms: July–September (peak midsummer)
- Bee/butterfly value: EXCELLENT (beloved by all)
- Placement: Middle
- Medicinal: Digestive, nervine, antimicrobial
- Varieties: Red, pink, purple

### [[Echinacea]] (Purple Coneflower)
- Height: 24–48 inches
- Flowers: Pink-purple with prominent orange cone center
- Blooms: July–October (very long season)
- Bee/butterfly value: EXCELLENT
- Placement: Middle to back
- Medicinal: Immune support, respiratory
- Hardiness: Very hardy perennial

### [[Lavender]]
- Height: 18–36 inches (varieties vary)
- Flowers: Purple spikes, fragrant
- Blooms: June–August
- Bee value: EXCELLENT (bees swarm lavender)
- Bee behavior: Watch bees dive into flowers
- Placement: Middle
- Medicinal: Calming, wound healing, antimicrobial
- Varieties: English (hardy), French (less hardy), Spanish

### [[White Yarrow]]
- Height: 24–36 inches
- Flowers: White/cream cluster flowers
- Blooms: June–September
- Bee/butterfly value: EXCELLENT
- Placement: Middle to back
- Medicinal: Wound healing, circulation, fever support
- Note: Available in multiple colors (use white for contrast)

## Supporting Pollinator Herbs

### [[Sage]]
- Flowers: Purple to pink spikes
- Bee value: Good (secondary bloomer)
- Placement: Middle
- Perennial: Long-lived, reliable

### [[Thyme]]
- Flowers: Small pink/purple/white flowers
- Bee value: Good (especially creeping varieties)
- Placement: Front border
- Abundant small flowers = multiple landing zones

### [[Oregano]]
- Flowers: Small pink/purple clustered
- Bee value: Moderate (small flowers)
- Bumblebee favorite: Heavier bees love oregano
- Placement: Front-middle

### [[Basil]] *(Annual, but prolific)*
- Flowers: Small white/pink flowers (if allowed to bolt)
- Bee value: Moderate-good (flowering varieties)
- Note: Most people pinch off flowers (culinary focus)
- Pollinator option: Let some bolt to flower

### [[Chamomile]]
- Flowers: Small daisy-like
- Bee value: Moderate (small flowers, some floral visitors)
- Placement: Front to middle
- Self-sower: Abundant if allowed

### [[Dill]]
- Flowers: Tiny yellow umbrella clusters (umbels)
- Bee value: EXCELLENT (especially beneficial insects)
- Parasitic wasp attraction: Supreme
- Placement: Middle to back
- Note: Tall, narrow form doesn't shade other plants

### [[Fennel]]
- Flowers: Tiny yellow umbels
- Bee value: EXCELLENT (especially beneficial insects)
- Placement: Back (tall)
- Similar to dill, fennel attracts parasitic wasps

## Color Strategy for Pollinator Appeal

### Bee Color Preferences
- **Blue/purple:** MOST attractive to bees
- **Yellow:** Very attractive
- **White:** Attractive
- **Red:** Less attractive (bees aren't great at seeing red)
- **Orange:** Moderate attraction

### Butterfly Preferences
- **Purple:** Very attractive
- **Pink:** Attractive
- **Orange:** Butterfly magnet
- **Yellow:** Attractive
- **White:** Moderate

### Design with Color
**Purple/Blue Focus:** Lavender, Echinacea, Sage, Hyssop (bee paradise)
**Pink/Red:** Bergamot varieties (butterfly + bee attraction)
**Yellow:** Dill, Fennel (beneficial insect heaven)
**White:** Yarrow (creates bright contrast)

## 📐 Pollinator Bed Design (6' x 12')

```
BACK (NORTH)
[Echinacea] [Dill] [Fennel] [Borage-tall]

MIDDLE
[Bergamot]  [Sage]  [Lavender]  [Lavender]

FRONT (SOUTH)
[Thyme] [Oregano] [Chamomile] [Yarrow-shorter form]
```

**Total plants:** 13–15

**Color progression:** 
- Back: Purple (Echinacea) + Yellow (Dill/Fennel)
- Middle: Pink (Bergamot) + Purple (Lavender, Sage)
- Front: Whites + smaller colorful

**Bloom sequence:** June–October (continuous blooms)

## Creating Ideal Pollinator Habitat

### Flower Density
- Plant densely enough for visual reward (bees see flowers from distance)
- Space for mature size (crowding = poor air, disease)
- Odd groupings (3, 5, 7 same plant) = better than scattered

### Height Layering
Helps bees navigate:
- Back: Tall flowers (Borage, Dill, Fennel = landing zones)
- Middle: Medium access (Bergamot, Echinacea = platforms)
- Front: Low access (Thyme, Oregano = ground entry)

### Soil & Watering
- Well-prepared bed (healthy plants = better nectar)
- Regular but not excessive watering
- Healthy soil = robust growth = abundant flowers

### Avoid Pesticides
- **CRITICAL:** No synthetic pesticides (kill pollinators)
- Use organic methods only:
  - Hand-pick pests
  - Encourage beneficial predators
  - Neem oil (organic, less toxic)
  - Companion planting

## Supporting Different Pollinators

### Honeybees
- Prefer blue/purple flowers
- Need deep flowers or platform shapes
- Lavender, Echinacea, Borage = gold standard
- Consistent nectar sources (multiple flowers)

### Bumblebees
- Heavier than honeybees
- Need sturdy landing platforms
- Bergamot, Sage, Oregano (wider landing zones)
- Longer flower tubes acceptable

### Butterflies
- Prefer pink/red/purple
- Need clustered flowers (flat-tops = better)
- Bergamot (shaggy clusters = butterfly paradise)
- Yarrow (flat clusters)
- Echinacea (purple favorite)

### Beneficial Insects (Parasitic Wasps)
- Hover flies, lacewings, parasitic wasps
- Need small flower clusters
- Dill, Fennel (tiny umbels = favorite)
- Eliminates garden pests naturally

### Hoverflies
- Mimic bees (harmless)
- Love small flowers
- Fennel, Dill, Yarrow
- Beneficial predators

## Year-Round Pollinator Garden Care

### Spring (March–April)
- Prepare beds
- Plant new stock
- Do not deadhead fall flowers (overwintering insects inside)

### Early Summer (May–June)
- First flowers emerge
- Begin weekly observation (watch pollinators)
- Pinch back (if needed) for bushier growth
- Water regularly

### Peak Season (July–August)
- Maximum bloom
- Minimal intervention (let flowers be!)
- Dead-head spent flowers to encourage more
- Water regularly (flowers = thirsty)
- Resist pesticides completely

### Fall (September–October)
- Final blooms
- Reduce deadheading (let seed form)
- Skip hard pruning (insects need seed heads for overwintering)
- Leave dead stalks over winter

### Winter (November–February)
- Leave seed heads standing (insect overwintering sites)
- Don't clean up beds!
- Cavity-nesting bees overwinter in hollow stems
- Spring cleanup: Clear debris but preserve structure

---

## What NOT to Do

### Avoid
✗ Pesticides (kills pollinators)  
✗ Herbicides (kills flowering plants)  
✗ Double/heavy-petaled flowers (no nectar access)  
✗ Weed-wacking in bloom season (destroys flowers)  
✗ Over-tidy beds (insects need leaf litter)  
✗ Water-logging (poor bee habitat)  

---

## Observing Predator/Prey in Pollinator Garden

### Beneficial Observation
- parasitic wasps (tiny, harmless to humans)
- Hover flies (look like small bees, harmless)
- Lacewings (eat aphids, etc.)
- Harvestmen (eat pest eggs)

### These Are Your Allies
- Presence = sign of healthy ecosystem
- They control garden pests naturally
- Eliminate need for pesticides
- Create resilient growing system

---

## Pollinator Garden Growth Expectations

### Year 1
- Some bloom first year
- Perennials establish roots (less flower energy)
- Building ecosystem
- Moderate pollinator activity

### Year 2
- Full bloom from perennials
- Self-sown Borage, Chamomile, Dill appear
- Increased pollinator activity
- Very rewarding!

### Year 3+
- Peak production
- Pollinator-dependent plants thriving
- Seed set excellent (more next-year volunteers)
- Garden feels "alive"
