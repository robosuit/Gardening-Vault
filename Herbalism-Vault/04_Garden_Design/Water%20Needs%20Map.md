# 💧 Water Needs Map

Organize your garden by moisture requirements, grouping plants with similar water needs to create efficient, low-stress watering zones.

---

## Why Group by Water Needs?

**Benefits:**
- Use drip irrigation or soaker hoses on single zone
- Prevent over-watering low-water plants
- Prevent under-watering moisture-loving plants
- Save water + effort
- Reduce plant stress + disease

---

## 🏜️ Low-Water Zone (Drought Tolerant)

**Watering schedule:** Establish 1–2 months, then minimal (natural rainfall)

**Best placement:** Raised beds, sandy soil, sunny exposed areas

### [[Thyme]]
- Water: Once established, very drought tolerant
- Soil: Sandy, well-drained
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Natural rainfall after establishment
- Concern: Wet feet = root rot; ensure drainage

### [[Oregano]]
- Water: Drought tolerant once stable
- Soil: Well-drained, lean soil okay
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Natural rainfall sufficient
- Benefit: Flavor intensifies with water stress (within reason)

### [[Rosemary]] *(if growing from kitchen spice or purchase)*
- Water: Very drought tolerant
- Soil: Sandy, well-drained essential
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Minimal; prefers dry
- Note: Worst case for wet soil; ensure perfect drainage

### [[Sage]]
- Water: Drought tolerant
- Soil: Well-drained
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Natural rainfall okay
- Concern: Won't thrive in soggy soil

### [[Lavender]]
*(especially important)*
- Water: Drought tolerant (established)
- Soil: Sandy, well-drained, NOT rich
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Natural rainfall after first season
- **CRITICAL:** Over-watering kills more Lavender than under-watering

### [[Marjoram]]
- Water: Tolerates dryness
- Soil: Well-drained
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Natural rainfall after establishment

## 💧 Medium-Water Zone

**Watering schedule:** Regular (1–2 inches per week), consistent

**Best placement:** Standard garden beds, mulched areas

### [[Basil]] (All varieties)
- Water: Regular, consistent moisture
- Soil: Rich, well-draining
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Don't let dry out; weekly check
- Note: Annual; replant each spring

### [[Echinacea]]
- Water: Medium (prefers drier than some, lusher than others)
- Soil: Well-drained
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Weekly during heat
- Note: Tolerates some drought once established

### [[Yarrow]] / [[White Yarrow]]
- Water: Medium; fine with some dryness
- Soil: Well-drained
- Sun: Full sun
- Frequency: Moderate; drought tolerant established

### [[Bergamot]]
- Water: Medium to moist-preferring
- Soil: Average to rich, consistent moisture nice
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Regular watering during dry spells
- Note: Dries out slower in partial shade

### [[Lemon Balm]]
- Water: Medium; prefers consistent
- Soil: Average to rich
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade (shade = lusher in heat)
- Frequency: Regular, consistent moisture
- Note: Gets leggy/flopping in too-dry conditions

### [[Chamomile]]
- Water: Medium
- Soil: Average
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Don't let get bone dry
- Note: Self-seeds freely if you let it dry between waterings

### [[Cilantro]]
- Water: Medium
- Soil: Rich, consistent moisture
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Regular; bolts quickly if dry or hot
- Note: Cool-season herb; extra water in spring/fall

---

## 🌊 Moist-Soil Zone

**Watering schedule:** Consistently moist (but not waterlogged)

**Best placement:** Lower garden areas, shade areas, amended soil with compost

### [[Peppermint]]
- Water: Moist; loves water
- Soil: Rich, moist
- Sun: Partial shade okay
- Frequency: Keep consistently moist
- **CRITICAL:** Plant in containers or with barriers (aggressive spreader)
- Note: Shade helps prevent drying out; benefits from afternoon shade

### [[Lemon Mint]]
- Water: Moist
- Soil: Rich, consistent moisture
- Sun: Partial shade
- Frequency: Regular watering
- **CRITICAL:** Container or barrier (mint family spreader)

### [[Mountain Mint]]
- Water: Moist
- Soil: Consistent moisture
- Sun: Partial shade okay
- Frequency: Regular watering
- **CRITICAL:** Container or barrier (aggressive spreader)

### [[Catnip]]
- Water: Medium to moist
- Soil: Average to moist
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Regular
- Note: Tolerates cat damage but appreciates moisture

### [[Horehound]]
- Water: Medium to moist
- Soil: Average to moist
- Sun: Full sun to partial shade
- Frequency: Regular
- Note: Less aggressive spreading than mints; can be open-bed

## 📊 Water Needs by Life Cycle

### Annuals (Extra Water Needed)
[[Basil]], [[Cilantro]], [[Dill]], [[Anise]], [[Borage]], [[Chamomile]]

**Why:** Shallow root systems, don't establish deep roots for drought access

**Watering:** Weekly minimum, more during heat

**Mulch:** Help retain moisture

---

### Perennials (Establish, Then Less Water)
[[Lavender]], [[Sage]], [[Oregano]], [[Thyme]], [[Echinacea]], etc.

**Year 1:** Regular watering (establish root systems)

**Year 2+:** Reduce to natural rainfall for low-water types

**Mulch:** Helps transition to low-water

---

## Setting Up Efficient Watering

### Drip Irrigation Zones

**Zone 1 (Low Water):** Thyme, Oregano, Sage, Lavender
- Drip line or soaker hose with timer
- Set: Water 1x weekly or less
- Time: Early morning (before heat)

**Zone 2 (Medium Water):** Basil, Echinacea, Bergamot, Lemon Balm
- Drip line or soaker hose
- Set: Water 2–3x weekly during growth
- Adjust: More during heat waves, less during cool/rainy

**Zone 3 (Moist):** Mints (in containers), Peppermint
- Hand watering or consistent drip
- Set: Check daily, water if top inch dry
- Note: Mints in containers dry faster

## Seasonal Watering Adjustments

### Spring (March–May)
**Focus:** Establishing new plants
- Water more frequently than summer (cooler = less evaporation)
- New perennials: 2–3x weekly
- Annuals: Plant after frost, then 2–3x weekly

### Summer (June–August)
**Focus:** Peak heat stress
- Increase frequency (high evaporation)
- Low-water plants: 1–2x weekly if extended drought
- Medium/moist: 3–4x weekly typical
- Early morning (6–8am) optimal (less evaporation)
- Mulch helps retain moisture

### Fall (September–November)
**Focus:** Slowing growth, rain increases
- Reduce frequency as temps cool
- Rainfall usually supplements
- Reduce annuals (approaching season end)
- Perennials: Less water (preparing dormancy)

### Winter (December–February)
**Focus:** Dormancy
- Minimal watering
- Perennials dormant (need very little)
- Rare: Snow/rain provides moisture
- Don't water in freezing conditions

## Mulching for Water Retention

### Benefits of Mulch
✓ Maintains soil moisture (30–50% reduction in water loss)  
✓ Regulates soil temperature  
✓ Reduces weeds  
✓ Improves soil as it breaks down  
✓ Makes garden look finished  

### Best Mulches for Herbs
- **Straw (not hay):** Ideal, breaks down, adds organic matter
- **Wood chips:** Good, take longer to break down
- **Aged compost:** Excellent, becomes soil
- **Leaves:** Free, break down beautifully

### Not Recommended for Most Herbs
✗ Fresh grass clippings (mat down, exclude air)  
✗ Plastic (blocks air, overheats soil)  
✗ Peat moss (dries out, hard to re-wet)  

### Apply 2–3 inches, keeping away from plant stems (prevents rot)

## Soil Amendments for Water Management

### For Low-Water Zones
**Add to improve drainage:**
- Sand (increases drainage)
- Perlite (lightweight, drains)
- Raised beds (natural drainage)

**Result:** Faster draining = suitable for drought-tolerant herbs

### For Moist-Soil Zones
**Add to improve water retention:**
- Compost (holds moisture)
- Aged manure (retains water)
- Peat moss (excellent retention)
- Lower elevation/basin (natural moisture sink)

**Result:** Slower draining = suitable for moisture-lovers

## Problem Diagnosis: Too Much/Too Little Water

### Signs of Over-Watering
- Yellowing leaves (not from nutrient lack)
- Drooping despite moist soil
- Root rot smell (musty)
- Fungal issues (powdery mildew)
- Leaves dropping
- Plant collapse looking lush but dying

### Signs of Under-Watering
- Wilting even after watering
- Crispy leaf edges
- Slow growth
- Smaller leaves than expected
- Premature flowering (stress response)
- Complete collapse when very stressed
